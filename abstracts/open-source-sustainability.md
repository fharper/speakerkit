# The honest path to open source sustainability

There is not a single piece of software out there that doesn't rely on open source in one way or another. Yet behind every open source product is a hard question most companies eventually face: how do you keep the lights on without closing the source?

Some companies choose to go proprietary. TinyMCE chose not to. With over 20 years as an open-source project, TinyMCE has navigated the real tension between openness and sustainability: from adopting an open-core model with premium plugins, to making the difficult decision to switch its license from MIT to GPL. A move that didn't please everyone, cost some integration with other projects, and sparked genuine debate in the community. But it's also a big part of what kept TinyMCE alive, and still free to use in open-source projects like Moodle and Joomla. This talk is not about why open source matters; you already know it's the way to go. It's about how to make it work as a business, based on the hard-won experience of doing exactly that.

## Elevator pitch

Open-source companies face a sustainability trap: grow the project, attract users, then struggle to generate enough revenue to keep it going, leading to the tempting but often damaging decision to close the source. This session addresses how to break that cycle without betraying your community or abandoning your open-source principles.

There are three key things attendees will learn for this talk:

1. How to structure an open-source business model that actually works: what to keep free, what to monetize, and how to draw that line without alienating your community.
2. How to navigate a license change: when it's the right call, how to communicate it, and how to manage the fallout since not everyone will agree.
3. How to bring your community through hard decisions: how to communicate changes that will upset some users, manage the friction, and retain long-term trust without walking back decisions that were right for the project.

By using TinyMCE's real timeline as a case study of 20+ years of decisions, pivots, and tradeoffs, the presentation will show concrete examples of the MIT to GPL license switch we have done, including community reactions. Most talks on open-source sustainability are either theoretical or told from a single contributor's point of view. The latest being important, this one is neither. It's a company sharing decisions that were genuinely difficult, publicly contested, and had real consequences: backed by real data and lived experience, this is not a product pitch. It's a story that I'll tell honestly, including what didn't go smoothly. TinyMCE is not a startup; it's a 20-year-old project that keeps evolving precisely to stay open and relevant.
